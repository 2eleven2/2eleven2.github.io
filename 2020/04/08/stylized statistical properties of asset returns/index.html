stylized statistical properties of asset returns

2020-04-08

Stylized fact

  1. Absence of autocorrelation: (linear) autocorelationships of asset returns are often insignificant, except for very smal intraday time scales (20 mintues) for which microstructure effets come into play.
  2. Heavy tails: the (unconditional) distribution of returns seems to diaplay a power-law or Pareto-like tail, with a tail index which is finite, higher than two and less than five for most data sets studies. In particular this excludes stable laws with infinite variance and the normal distribution. However the precise form of the tails is difficult to determine.
  3. Gain/loss asymmetry: one observes large drawdowns in stock prices and stock index values but not equally large upward movements.
  4. Aggregational Gaussianity: as one increases the time scale $\Delta t$ over which returns are calculated, their distribution looks more and more like a normal distribution. In particular, the shape of the distribution is not the same at different time scales.
  5. Intermittency: returns display, at any time scale, a high degree of variability. This is quantified by the presense of irregular bursts in time sereis of a wide variety of volatility estimators.
  6. Volatility clustering: different measures of volatility display a positive autocorrelation over several days, which quantifies the fact that high-volatility events tend to cluster in time.
  7. Conditional heavy tails: even after correcting returns for volatility clustering(e.g. via GARCH-type models), the residual time sereis still exhibit heavy tails. However, the tails are less heavy than in the unconditional distribution of returns.
  8. Slow decay of autocorrelation in absolute returns: the autocorrelation function of absolute returns decays slowly as a function of hte time lag, roughly as a power law with an exponent $\beta \in [0.2, 0.4]$. This is sometimes interpreted as a sign of long-range dependence.
  9. Leverage effect: most measures of volatility of an asset are negatively correlated with the returns of that asset.
  10. Volume/volatility correlation: trading volume is correlated with all measures of volatility.
  11. Asymmetry in time scales: coarse-grained measure of volatility predict fine-scale volatility better than the other way round.
